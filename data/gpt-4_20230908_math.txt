**5.2 Derivatives**

The concept of the derivative is at the heart of differential calculus. At a basic level, a derivative measures how a function changes as its input changes. Imagine driving a car, and as you observe the speedometer, you're essentially observing a derivative: the rate of change of distance with respect to time.

**5.2.1 Definition of the Derivative**

The derivative of a function \( f \) at a particular point \( x \) is defined as:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \]

If this limit exists, the function is said to be differentiable at that point.

**5.2.2 Basic Derivative Rules**

* **Power Rule:** For any real number \( n \), 
\[ \frac{d}{dx} [x^n] = nx^{(n-1)} \]
  
* **Sum Rule:** If \( u \) and \( v \) are functions of \( x \), 
\[ \frac{d}{dx} [u(x) + v(x)] = u'(x) + v'(x) \]
  
* **Product Rule:** 
\[ \frac{d}{dx} [u(x) \cdot v(x)] = u(x) \cdot v'(x) + u'(x) \cdot v(x) \]
  
* **Quotient Rule:** If \( v(x) \neq 0 \), 
\[ \frac{d}{dx} \left[\frac{u(x)}{v(x)}\right] = \frac{u'(x) \cdot v(x) - u(x) \cdot v'(x)}{[v(x)]^2} \]

**5.2.3 Chain Rule**

The chain rule is used when differentiating composite functions. If \( y \) is a function of \( u \) and \( u \) is a function of \( x \), then:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \]

**5.2.4 Higher Order Derivatives**

The second derivative, \( f''(x) \), represents the rate of change of the rate of change of \( f \), which can be interpreted as the "acceleration" of the function. Similarly, third, fourth, and other higher-order derivatives can be taken.

**5.2.5 Applications of Derivatives**

Derivatives have numerous applications in physics, engineering, economics, biology, and other fields. Some of these applications include:

* **Optimization problems:** Finding the maximum or minimum values of functions.
* **Related rates:** Problems that involve relating the rates of change of two or more quantities.
* **Modeling motion:** Describing how objects move in terms of velocity, acceleration, etc.
* **Elasticity in economics:** Measuring the responsiveness of one variable to changes in another variable.

**3.3 Theorems in Geometry**

Geometry, which translates to "measurement of land" in Greek, is the branch of mathematics that studies the sizes, shapes, properties, and dimensions of objects and figures. Over the course of history, numerous theorems, or proven statements, have been developed that form the foundation of geometry.

**3.3.1 Pythagorean Theorem**

Perhaps the most famous geometric theorem, it states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle) is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides.

Mathematically:
\[ c^2 = a^2 + b^2 \]
where \( c \) is the hypotenuse, and \( a \) and \( b \) are the other two sides.

Historical accounts suggest that Pythagoras himself may not have come up with the theorem but instead, the knowledge was possibly already known to Babylonians. The theorem’s proof has been approached in numerous ways, with over 400 different proofs documented.

**3.3.2 Thales’ Theorem**

Thales, an ancient Greek mathematician, is credited with a simple yet pivotal idea: An angle inscribed in a semicircle is a right angle.

This theorem sets the foundation for many other geometric proofs. Thales is also known for another related postulate: If A, B, and C are points on a circle where line segment BC is a diameter of the circle, then angle BAC is a right angle.

**3.3.3 Euclid’s Elements and Postulates**

Euclid’s work, "Elements", is one of the most influential works in the history of mathematics, serving as the main textbook for teaching mathematics (particularly geometry) until the late 19th or early 20th century.

Euclid began with five postulates that he considered self-evident:

1. A straight line segment can be drawn between any two points.
2. Any straight line segment can be extended indefinitely in a straight line.
3. A circle can be drawn with any center and any radius.
4. All right angles are congruent.
5. (Parallel Postulate) If a line segment intersects two straight lines forming two interior angles on the same side that sum to less than two right angles, then the two lines, if extended indefinitely, will meet on that side.

From these postulates, Euclid derived many of the theorems we still study in geometry.

**3.3.4 Area and Volume Formulas**

While not theorems in the strictest sense, these formulas result from applying geometric principles:

* **Triangle Area:** \( A = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} \)
* **Circle Area:** \( A = \pi \times r^2 \) where \( r \) is the radius.
* **Rectangular Prism Volume:** \( V = l \times w \times h \) where \( l \) is length, \( w \) is width, and \( h \) is height.
* **Sphere Volume:** \( V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \)

**3.3.5 Circle Theorems**

There are many fascinating theorems related to circles:

* **Inscribed Angle Theorem:** The measure of an inscribed angle is half the measure of its intercepted arc.
* **Tangent-Radius Theorem:** A radius that is perpendicular to a tangent at the point of tangency.

**3.3.6 Advanced Theorems**

As geometry has evolved, especially with the advent of non-Euclidean geometries, there are numerous advanced theorems that dive into the properties and relationships of shapes in diverse geometric landscapes. These include theorems like Desargues' Theorem, Pappus's Hexagon Theorem, and more. Each of these plays a pivotal role in furthering the understanding and applications of geometry in both theoretical and practical arenas.

The beauty of geometry lies not just in the results or theorems but in the process of proof. The deductive nature of geometric proofs shows the rigorous logic and structure underlying the spatial world. This is just a brief dive into the vast ocean that is geometry, with many more theorems, postulates, and axioms that make up the intricate tapestry of this fascinating branch of mathematics.
